## Section 2 — The Buyer Problem

Audience: buyers.

Headline: You have a full wardrobe. You still have nothing to wear.

Three beats — write as editorial prose, not bullet points:

Beat 1 — The Pinterest gap:
You spend an hour on Pinterest saving outfits.
Then you open your wardrobe and none of it translates.
Different body. Different wardrobe. Different world.
The inspiration sits in a folder you never go back to.

Beat 2 — The algorithm problem:
You scroll Instagram and Facebook stores looking for one piece.
The algorithm shows you whoever paid for reach or already has
100,000 followers. The boutique with 300 followers and the
exact dress you want? Buried. You scroll for 45 minutes
and either settle or give up.

Beat 3 — The disappearing inventory:
You find something. It's perfect. It's a story.
You screenshot it, save the DM, tell yourself you'll come back.
Twenty-four hours later the post is gone.
The seller's contact is somewhere in your camera roll.
You never find it.

Visual: triptych — a Pinterest board, an Instagram scroll,
a camera roll full of dead screenshots.

## Section 4 — The Seller Problem

Audience: sellers.

Headline: Your best pieces deserve more than 24 hours
         and more than the algorithm allows.

Beat 1 — The disappearing storefront:
You post on Instagram. Your story disappears in 24 hours.
The customer who would have bought it never saw it.
Meanwhile that piece sits unsold.

Beat 2 — The reach problem:
Facebook and Instagram surface whoever has the most followers.
If you are a small seller with a genuinely great eye,
the algorithm does not care.
You are invisible to the exact women who would love your work.

Beat 3 — The waste:
Unsold stock does not just cost money.
Eventually it gets bagged, exported, or burned.
That is the real price of broken discovery.
